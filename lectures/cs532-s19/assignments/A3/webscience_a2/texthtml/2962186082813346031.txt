The Times view on Amnesty: in crisis
An important organisation with a ‘toxic’ culture needs an urgent rethink
Few organisations are as essential as Amnesty International. Founded in 1961 by a British lawyer, Peter Benenson, Amnesty’s mission was to publicise people who were being persecuted around the world who otherwise might have been forgotten. To the man or woman in a prison cell, to the victim of torture, to the citizen waiting for the dawn knock of the secret police, Amnesty became a lifeline to the outside world. In its half century of existence, Amnesty hasn’t got everything right but its work has informed almost all campaigns for human rights. Consequently it has become a thorn in the side of authoritarian regimes and human rights abusers everywhere. They wish Amnesty ill when democrats wish it well.
