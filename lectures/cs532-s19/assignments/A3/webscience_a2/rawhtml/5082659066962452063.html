A few things spring to mind.

Trump lacks certain qualities which the British traditionally esteem.

For instance, he has no class, no charm, no coolness, no credibility, no compassion, no wit, no warmth, no wisdom, no subtlety, no sensitivity, no self-awareness, no humility, no honour and no grace - all qualities, funnily enough, with which his predecessor Mr. Obama was generously blessed.

So for us, the stark contrast does rather throw Trumps limitations into embarrassingly sharp relief.

Plus, we like a laugh. And while Trump may be laughable, he has never once said anything wry, witty or even faintly amusing - not once, ever.

I dont say that rhetorically, I mean it quite literally: not once, not ever. And that fact is particularly disturbing to the British sensibility - for us, to lack humour is almost inhuman.

But with Trump, its a fact. He doesnt even seem to understand what a joke is - his idea of a joke is a crass comment, an illiterate insult, a casual act of cruelty.

Trump is a troll. And like all trolls, he is never funny and he never laughs; he only crows or jeers.

And scarily, he doesnt just talk in crude, witless insults - he actually thinks in them. His mind is a simple bot-like algorithm of petty prejudices and knee-jerk nastiness.

There is never any under-layer of irony, complexity, nuance or depth. Its all surface.

Some Americans might see this as refreshingly upfront.

Well, we dont. We see it as having no inner world, no soul.

And in Britain we traditionally side with David, not Goliath. All our heroes are plucky underdogs: Robin Hood, Dick Whittington, Oliver Twist.

Trump is neither plucky, nor an underdog. He is the exact opposite of that.

Hes not even a spoiled rich-boy, or a greedy fat-cat.

Hes more a fat white slug. A Jabba the Hutt of privilege.

And worse, he is that most unforgivable of all things to the British: a bully.

That is, except when he is among bullies; then he suddenly transforms into a snivelling sidekick instead.

There are unspoken rules to this stuff - the Queensberry rules of basic decency - and he breaks them all. He punches downwards - which a gentleman should, would, could never do - and every blow he aims is below the belt. He particularly likes to kick the vulnerable or voiceless - and he kicks them when they are down.

So the fact that a significant minority - perhaps a third - of Americans look at what he does, listen to what he says, and then think 'Yeah, he seems like my kind of guy is a matter of some confusion and no little distress to British people, given that:

  1. Americans are supposed to be nicer than us, and mostly are.
  2. You don't need a particularly keen eye for detail to spot a few flaws in the man.

This last point is what especially confuses and dismays British people, and many other people too; his faults seem pretty bloody hard to miss.

After all, its impossible to read a single tweet, or hear him speak a sentence or two, without staring deep into the abyss. He turns being artless into an art form; he is a Picasso of pettiness; a Shakespeare of shit. His faults are fractal: even his flaws have flaws, and so on ad infinitum.

God knows there have always been stupid people in the world, and plenty of nasty people too. But rarely has stupidity been so nasty, or nastiness so stupid.

He makes Nixon look trustworthy and George W look smart.

In fact, if Frankenstein decided to make a monster assembled entirely from human flaws - he would make a Trump.

And a remorseful Doctor Frankenstein would clutch out big clumpfuls of hair and scream in anguish:

'My God what have I created?

If being a twat was a TV show, Trump would be the boxed set.

Lets ignore the first premise, because it simply isnt true. The British are well known for being quite reserved and not speaking out when when things are very wrong. Theyll eat burned restaurant food and pay for it rather than complain.

On the second count, I can speak only for myself.

I used to look at Trump as a famous and wealthy businessman who had written a very famous book about how to become successful. Reports of his activities were infrequent in the UK, but most people still knew his name, something about his private life, Ivana, Ivanka, etc., and about his bankruptcies too - and that he somehow survived and ended up still rich.

The politics of Democrat vs Republican in the US is not generally well understood in the UK. The complexity of interaction between Presidency, Congress, Senate, Federal and State lawmaking, Judiciary - its all far more complicated than our Parliamentary system. Many people in the UK wouldnt know whether a past POTUS was Democrat or Republican. In that sense, were pretty much ambivalent about the politics of the President, and its his actions and words as a person we see and make judgment upon.

When Trump ran for President, most people here had no opinion of him.

As his rallies intensified, and his rhetoric became more public, some of us were sympathetic to him when he was attacked for certain statements and behaviours. I heard a lot of argument for and against his ideas, behaviour, and presentation.

There came a point when, I recall, the attitude turned. He no longer got the benefit of the doubt, because what wed thought were misinterpretations of his words and actions turned out to be the truth - because he cleared up the confusion by clearly re-stating the contentious things.

I started to look at his past behaviour, interviews, writings, and became aware that this man had done harm to a lot of people in the past, basically by being a hard-nosed bully. He even boasted of it. Its not a personality type we like in the UK.

I started to listen to full versions of his campaign speeches, and frankly couldnt find any real content. There was a lot of rabble-rousing invective like Lock her up and Hillary is a crook and Were gonna build a wall and personal insults against everyone else involved in the race to the White House. There was no real substance, no philosophy of HOW America would be great again, just the boast that only Trump could make it that way. There was a LOT of personal aggrandisement. Only I can[insert any claim you like] and Im the best at [insert any activity] and Many people [never named] say Donald Trump is a genius.

In the UK, that kind of political speech making would make the man a laughing stock. The imitating of a disabled person would end his campaign within a day. The not-so-veiled calls to his supporters to resort to violence against detractors, likewise. He would probably have been questioned by the Police for incitement. We all watched, gobsmacked, as he not only got away with it, but USED it to publicise himself.

We already knew about his attitude in the golf club business in Scotland. We dislike that kind of my money is more powerful than your argument attitude. He made no attempt, not has he ever, to be NICE. He made hateful statements, repeated them, inflated them further, lied, made the lies bigger, pretended he never said things that he was on video saying in front of international cameras, and called the reporting of it fake. He still does.

Whats to like?

Trump doesnt care what we think. Hes a sociopath.

Well, to answer that question in what I think is the appropriate spirit:

  1. The British are not infamous for saying what they think. If they are infamous for anything, its snobbery and colonialism.
  2. British and specifically English manners are to avoid saying what you think, so as not to offend the other person, but to more or less inadvertently offend the person anyway, by making it very clear what you think by means of various kinds of subtle and not-so-subtle non-verbal behaviour which the other person is at a loss to respond to. This is what the British have instead of martial arts.
  3. The idea that the British are straight-talking no-nonsense types is unfamiliar to anyone who has spent time among them.
  4. Saying what you think is not necessarily a virtue. In a statesman, its an absolute liability, unless the statesman actively wants his country to become less powerful and more vulnerable.
  5. Donald Trump is not likeable by virtue of the fact that he says what he thinks, although much of the time he clearly does say what he thinks. The trouble is that when he does say what he thinks, it becomes clear that he doesnt think very hard, and that the little that he thinks is generally stupid, uninformed, racist, sexist, bigoted, cruel, vindictive or some or all of the above.

Despite their many flaws, the British are on balance working pretty hard to be better citizens of the worldwell, not of Europe, but everybody makes mistakesand, as such, they have little patience with the generally tenth-rate nature of Donald Trump as both a politician and as a human.

Giving you the benefit of the doubt I have edited your question to replace infamouos with famous based on what Merriam Webster says.

Yes, famous and infamous have some similarities (both words are descended from the Latin fama, meaning fame, and both often have to do with being well-known), but they have decidedly different meanings. Famous typically carries the meaning of widely known, and is often used in a positive manner; infamous, on the other hand, has a negative set of meanings, such as having a reputation of the worst kind or causing or bringing infamy.

I did not report your question on the off chance you sincerely want answers, dont know the meaning of infamous, and are not just stirring up the pot. On the other hand you may actually mean infamous in which case your question is insincere and should be reported since it contains a statement of fact not in evidence (that the British are infamous in some way) and violates BNBR too for being disrespectful of the British.
